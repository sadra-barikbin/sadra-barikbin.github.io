There is a particular kind of forgetting that only happens to people who don&rsquo;t
keep notebooks. It is not the loss of facts &mdash; facts are cheap now, a search
away. It is the loss of *attention*: the exact slant of an afternoon, the thing a
stranger said that you meant to remember, the shape of a thought before it hardened
into an opinion.

I started keeping a notebook the way most people do, which is to say badly and
inconsistently. The early entries embarrass me. But somewhere along the way the
habit stopped being about producing anything and became a way of *noticing* &mdash;
of treating my own days as worth a second look.

> We forget all too soon the things we thought we could never forget.

That line has followed me for years. It is the whole argument for the notebook in a
single sentence. Not the diary&rsquo;s ledger of events, but the keeping of small,
useless, irreplaceable things.

---

So this is where some of them go now. Not because they are important &mdash; most of
them aren&rsquo;t &mdash; but because the act of writing them down is a way of saying:
*I was here, and I was paying attention.*

That seems like enough reason to me.
